Solar System - Earth's Home

Sun

The Sun is the star at the center of our solar system. It is the largest object in our solar system and the closest star to Earth. The Sun is a yellow dwarf star that is responsible for the heat and light that makes life on Earth possible.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system, orbiting closest to the Sun, making it the fastest planet in its orbit;. It has a rocky, cratered surface with almost no atmosphere, and due to its proximity to the sun, experiences extreme temperature fluctuations between day and night. Notably, Mercury has no moons and is named after the Roman messenger god known for his speed

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun, often called Earth's "twin" due to its similar size, but with a scorching hot surface thanks to a thick, carbon dioxide-rich atmosphere that traps heat, making it the hottest planet in our solar system;. It is the brightest planet visible from Earth, appearing as a brilliant "evening star" due to its thick clouds of sulfuric acid;.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun, and the only known planet to support life, with a surface primarily covered by water, making it a "blue planet.". It has a single moon and is surrounded by an atmosphere that protects it from harmful solar radiation, allowing diverse ecosystems to flourish across its landmasses and oceans

Moon

Luna, the Latin name for "Moon," is Earth's only natural satellite, orbiting our planet and reflecting sunlight to appear as a bright celestial body in the night sky; its phases, from crescent to full, are visible due to the changing angle of sunlight hitting its surface, and its gravitational pull significantly influences the Earth's tides, creating rhythmic cycles of high and low water levels.

About our solar system

About solar system

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of the Sun and the objects that orbit it. It formed about 4.6 billion years ago when a dense region of a molecular cloud collapsed, forming the Sun and a protoplanetary disc. The Sun is a typical star that maintains a balanced equilibrium by the fusion of hydrogen into helium at its core, releasing this energy from its outer photosphere. Astronomers classify it as a G-type main-sequence star

Planets

The largest objects that orbit the Sun are the eight planets. In order from the Sun, they are four terrestrial planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars); two gas giants (Jupiter and Saturn); and two ice giants (Uranus and Neptune). All terrestrial planets have solid surfaces. Inversely, all giant planets do not have a definite surface, as they are mainly composed of gases and liquids. Over 99.86% of the Solar System's mass is in the Sun and nearly 90% of the remaining mass is in Jupiter and Saturn.

Formation

The Solar System formed at least 4.568 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a region within a large molecular cloud. This initial cloud was likely several light-years across and probably birthed several stars. As is typical of molecular clouds, this one consisted mostly of hydrogen, with some helium, and small amounts of heavier elements fused by previous generations of stars.
